A Note Before This Story Begins

from Momo

Before this story begins, I want you to know how it begins.

The earliest memories in these pages did not come to me through photographs or stories told by others. They came to me through past life regression, a guided meditation I practiced many times, following the work of Dr. Brian Weiss. In one of those sessions, I found myself going back further than I ever had before. Back to being a baby. I know this because I was looking up at the world from the floor, at yellow plastic woven chairs, at white tile, at the light coming through a window. I saw the room completely and described everything I saw to my mother. She confirmed it without hesitation. That was our first house. Everything I had seen was real.

I offer this not as proof of anything. Only as honesty about where these pages come from. Some memories arrive through language. These arrived through something older than that.

This is not a story. It is not a fable. It is simply the truth of a life, told as honestly as I know how.

Room I
the beginning of awareness

The First Room

The Beginning of Awareness

From the first moment I began to see, I knew nothing of the word “me,” so it was easy to simply be. I can still remember my first room, though I couldn’t have been more than a few months old. The world towered above me... legs and light and laughter seen from the floor up. There were yellow plastic woven chairs, so cheery and geometrically fascinating from my tiny perspective. The floors were hard and white, cool against my skin, and the warmth of my mother’s arms when she lifted me felt like sunlight turned to flesh. Beams of real sunlight danced through the window like playful fairies as I snuggled my little silkie and devoured my warm bottle of milk. These memories still drift vividly through my mind’s eye whenever I reach for them.

Time then was not a straight line but a shimmer... moments floating, colliding, vanishing like soap bubbles. I remember sound before words: the rhythm of voices, the hum of a refrigerator that felt like a lullaby, the faraway bark of a dog echoing through sunlight. The air itself seemed alive, carrying warmth, scent, and comfort in invisible waves. I didn’t yet know what distance was, or that people could ever leave. Everything was here, always; mother, light, the gentle thud of her heart when she held me. The world was a cradle, and I floated inside it, content to simply exist.

Then one morning, the rhythm changed. My mother’s arms lifted me not toward the window light but toward the door. I remember the rustle of her clothes, the scent of her perfume, the soft tremor in my chest as we stepped into the brightness. The world outside moved too fast... cars flashing by, trees bending in the wind, clouds racing overhead. I didn’t know the word “leaving,” only the ache of motion and the blur of color as we drove away. But soon the road slowed, and the air grew calmer. My grandmother’s little white house appeared, wrapped in flowers and sunlight, as if it had been waiting for me all along. Her doorway smelled of coffee and goodness. Inside, everything glowed; the toasty air, the hum of her voice, the small white poodle Tiki wagging as if he’d known me forever, Lucy the orange cat stretching on the windowsill, and Bridget, soft and bright, resting at the foot of grandma’s chair. My fear dissolved in that warmth. My mother’s goodbye faded into the rhythm of my grandmother’s love, steady as a heartbeat.

Behind my grandmother’s house was her garden, a tiny patch of earth that seemed to me as vast as a forest. Flowers bloomed in every corner, especially pansies, her favorite, their faces bright and brave in the sun. The air was always alive with wings and motion... bees murmuring, butterflies tumbling, birds singing from the cherry tree that guarded the fence. My grandmother never hurried me. She moved through her days with the patience of the earth itself, speaking softly, laughing easily, never cross, never cruel. She’d tell me that silence was golden, and in her company, I learned what that meant. We’d walk together to the grocery store or down toward the flood wall, her hand warm in mine, her voice drifting with stories from when she was a girl during the great flood in Parkersburg. Even the way she said the word “flood” carried a hush, as if the river still lived inside her memory.

I remember lying on the ground near the rhubarb, Bridget and Tiki and Charlie and Ty somewhere close by, the sun warm on my back, watching the ants. They were all my friends, my truest ones in those early years, the dogs and cats who asked nothing of me and gave everything. We were our own little world out there, the animals and I, while my grandmother moved quietly nearby with her flowers. I would watch the ants for hours. Over days. Over weeks. They moved in perfect order through their own complete universe, purposeful and unhurried, carrying things many times their size, communicating without words, building something I couldn’t see the whole of but knew was there. I would lie very still and imagine myself small enough to walk among them, to enter their world the way you step through a door. I understood something then that I wouldn’t have language for until much later, that the world was layered. That life existed in worlds within worlds, each one whole and busy and entirely unaware of the others running alongside it. That you could be standing in one world and another could be happening right beneath your feet, or across an ocean, or in a life you hadn’t lived yet. I think of those hours sometimes when I try to understand how I was able, later, to step off planes in countries where I didn’t speak a single word and feel curious rather than only afraid. Something in me had already practiced it. Had already learned, on my grandmother’s back steps with her animals and her flowers and her silence, that other worlds were not threatening. They were just waiting to be understood.

The kitchen was its own small world. A percolator sat on the stove, sighing and bubbling with a sound I came to love before I even knew the word for it. She always made her coffee with lots of cream and sugar, and she always gave me a little taste. I didn’t know then that she was giving me something I would carry for the rest of my life. I only knew it was warm, and sweet, and hers.

But it was the back steps where she truly taught me. They weren’t much, just a few steps leading down from the house into her garden, but to me they were a throne and a classroom and a church all at once. She would sit there and take my hand, this tiny woman, under five feet tall, and we would look at her flowers together without needing to say very much at all. She moved through silence the way other people moved through conversation, easily, naturally, as if quiet were simply another way of speaking. I learned from her, without being taught, that you could transmit entire worlds without a single word. That stillness was not emptiness. That if you sat still long enough and opened yourself completely, the garden would come to you.

Every time I was with her, I felt I was coming home. Not to a place, but to something. A feeling I recognized before I had language for it, something settled and safe and completely without performance. I have never in my life met anyone as genuinely pure as she was. Not before, not since. I was lucky in ways I couldn’t possibly have understood then, that my first and deepest experience of love came without condition, without shadow. She was simply good. The way sunlight is good. The way a flower turning toward warmth is good. It became the measure I would carry quietly inside me for everything that came after.

Her patience had been honed by a lifetime of love and loss. Motherhood came easily to my grandmother; she had raised six children over twenty years, from the eldest to the youngest, my mother, the baby of the family. When I was born, my grandfather’s health had already fallen into decline, and he was bedridden. He called me Moni-Kay, and somehow, even in his frailty, I brought a spark of joy back into the quiet house. Their nest had just emptied after a lifetime of fullness, children grown, married, and gone, and now, suddenly, a baby’s cry filled the silence again.

My mother was not yet nineteen, newly married, and shouldering more than most could bear: a demanding husband, a new job, a newborn daughter, and a dying father. When my grandfather passed away eight days after my first birthday, her temporary position with the City of Parkersburg became full-time. It seemed the right moment to give my grandmother something... or rather, someone... to keep her heart and hands joyfully busy. She accepted without hesitation, quietly and with her characteristic grace. From then on, I was hers each day of the week until kindergarten finally pulled me away. Those years became the golden thread of my early life and the essence of who I am still today.

Room II
awareness turned outward

A Room with Windows

Awareness Turned Outward

There had always been a gentle balance between my mother’s presence and my grandmother’s arms, a peaceful rhythm I moved between without effort. But as I grew, new windows began to open, and through them I sensed that not everything was as harmonious as it had once seemed.

The contrast revealed itself most sharply when my father was near. Something in the air shifted then, not only between him and me, but between him and my mother as well. Where warmth usually lived, there was an absence I could not name. I felt it as a hollow space, a longing to experience with him the same ease and closeness I knew with my mother and grandmother. Instead, there was a wall. I did not yet understand words like jealousy or resentment, only that I tried quietly and earnestly to be seen.

I wanted him to recognize me as his little daughter, the one who looked so much like him, the one who needed his love to grow whole and unguarded. That same feeling lingered when his mother, who lived across the street, was present. A strain entered the otherwise cheerful home, a subtle heaviness that settled without explanation.

My father worked every day, and when he returned home for dinner, he often disappeared into the television, the glow of its screen replacing any attention he might have given me. He never spent time with me, and even then I sensed his resentment, my mother forced to choose between being with him or tending to me. I could not understand her position; I only felt the loss. And so I reached for her more, trying to fill the emptiness he left behind.

The two sides of my family could not have been more different. On one side, I was openly adored, surrounded by aunts and uncles who offered affection freely and without condition. Their love felt effortless, as natural as breathing. On the other side stood my father, his mother, and his sister, bound together less by warmth than by competition. Their unity appeared only when others were watching, preserved in photographs and public moments, sustained by appearances rather than intimacy. Even as a child, I sensed the hollowness beneath it.

I was too young to understand the reasons for their animosity toward one another, and even now, it remains something I cannot fully explain. But I felt it then, unmistakably. The contrast was sharp enough to teach me without words. I knew, instinctively, which world I belonged to. Somewhere deep within me, an awareness was already forming, guiding me toward the example of my mother’s family. I understood, without being told, that if I wanted happiness, if I wanted a sense of lightness in my life, this was the path I would have to follow.

That awareness did not stay abstract; it settled into the way I moved through the world. I became attentive, watchful, sensitive to shifts in tone and mood. I learned to adjust myself instinctively, to soften my presence, to be agreeable, helpful, easy to love. Praise felt like proof of safety, and I sought it quietly, hoping it might bridge the distance I felt. I tried not to need too much and instead turned toward a faith that had not been taught; it simply was.

My mother used to say that I had begun speaking very late as a child. Everyone worried at first. But once I finally found my voice, there was no stopping it. My little motor mouth turned on and stayed on. I talked constantly to her, to my grandmother, to my toys, to myself. Around those I trusted, words poured out of me effortlessly, as if I had been storing them up for years. But where affection felt uncertain, my voice disappeared. There, I became quiet and shy, careful with every sound I made.

My mother had given me a little plastic, glow-in-the-dark star with a photograph of Jesus inside. Each night, after the lights were out, I would watch it and pray about everything and anything until I fell into a deep sleep. I spoke to Jesus the way a child speaks to a friend, simply and without fear. I spoke to my guardian angels, to the archangels too, without knowing their names or roles, only that they were there and listening. No one had ever told me that I couldn’t speak directly to them, and so I did. This quiet ritual became a refuge, something I would carry with me for the rest of my life.

Only later, when I learned about religions and their doctrines, did I begin to question my methods… but not for long. They had worked for me perfectly, just as they always had. Funny enough, my father would return that little star to me many years later, and I cherish it now as one of my most prized possessions.

Around those I felt secure with, I was open and expressive, unguarded in my joy. But where affection felt uncertain, I became careful. I listened more than I spoke. I studied faces. I learned which version of myself was most welcome and carried it with me like a small offering. Even then, without language for it, I understood that love could be abundant or withheld, and I adjusted accordingly. This was how I learned to belong…by sensing first, then shaping myself to fit.

Room III
Learning to Belong

A Room Full of Voices

Learning to Belong

On a crisp, sunny September morning, I woke with a feeling I had never known before. It was excitement braided tightly with anxiety. My stomach fluttered as if butterflies might burst free before I was even dressed. It was my first day of school, the first time I would spend hours with children who were not my cousins or the few neighbors I knew.

I had lived a sheltered, gentle life, my days spent with my grandmother and my weekends with my mother or in the quiet company of my dolls and the great outdoors. I loved being an only child. I was never lonely. I didn’t yet know what loneliness meant.

Still, I had heard so much about this day that its arrival felt overwhelming. I could hardly eat breakfast, though I loved the little dress my mother had chosen for me. She tied my hair into pigtails with ribbons and was almost as excited as I was.

We drove down the familiar road, but instead of passing my favorite hot dog place, Dave’s (where in West Virginia they serve hot dogs with sauce and slaw, still my favorite way to eat one), we turned onto a side road and climbed toward Lubeck Elementary School. Children were everywhere, running with backpacks and lunch boxes, laughing as they met friends they already knew. Parents kissed cheeks and hurried away. The air buzzed with voices.

They all seemed happy, and suddenly I was terrified.

I didn’t want to leave my mother. I didn’t want to enter that busy, echoing building. She took my hand and led me into the kindergarten classroom, where I met my teacher, Ms. Eades, whose sweet smile steadied me. Of all the teachers I would have in my life, she is the only one whose name I remember.

I was the only child sobbing when a very pretty little girl with long blonde hair and big blue eyes came to me and took my hand. She told me not to worry, that she would be my friend and that we would have fun together. Her name was Beth, and even now we still speak from time to time. Her kindness was the first I ever remember receiving from a schoolmate, and it opened this new room with gentleness instead of fear.

The first voice that filled our classroom once we had quieted was Ms. Eades leading us in the Pledge of Allegiance. We placed our tiny hands over our hearts and looked up at the red, white, and blue flag with its scatter of stars while she patiently taught us the words. Even now, when I hear the pledge at large gatherings, I feel again that small but sincere pride of belonging to something larger than myself.

Later that morning we were excused for recess. In those days we had one before lunch and another in the afternoon, and I had never heard such noise. Children shouted, laughed, argued, and ran in every direction at once. The chaos wrapped around me, and the old feeling of not fitting in returned. Everyone seemed to find their little groups so easily while I stood alone, unsure where to go. Once again Beth came to my rescue, taking my hand and introducing me to her friends. Through her kindness I learned my first lesson about cliques, that sometimes you were welcomed, and sometimes you were not. Slowly I began to find my way, a few small friendships that made the mornings worthwhile, even if I still preferred my quiet weekends with my grandmother.

School also taught me the games children carried home to the neighborhood. My favorite was hide-and-seek. We played it on the playground, but it was even better in the late afternoons before dinner, hiding behind fences and bushes while waiting for the call of “Olly Olly in free” that meant we could run back to base. My heart would pound when I was close to being caught or when I decided to dash for safety. These games filled my grade-school years with laughter and breathless joy, even as I always listened for the dinner bell, or for my father calling my name from up the hill, reminding me that evening and the rules of home were waiting too.

I also remember the first time another child laughed at me. It was a neighborhood boy I often played with, but this time he did it on the playground with my classmates watching. It was something small, perhaps the way I spoke too much, or the ribbons in my hair, or how tall and thin I was, or the clothes I wore that day. I never knew exactly why. But I remember the sound of the laughter and the heat rising to my cheeks. Until that moment, I had not known that giggles could make you feel suddenly different, as if a light had been turned on and everyone could see something about you that you did not understand yourself. I went quiet then, not because I had nothing to say, but because I was learning how carefully voices could be used. It was the first time I understood that kindness was not automatic, and it made me treasure Beth’s friendship, and every gentle voice, even more.

Slowly, in that room full of voices, I began to find my own. Not loudly, not all at once, but carefully, where kindness lived and where friendship made space for me. It was the beginning of learning how to belong, not by losing myself, but by listening, choosing, and speaking with care.

Room IV
The First Knowledge of Danger

A Room of Shadows

The First Knowledge of Danger

My elementary years come to me in fragments, but some memories still stand bright against the fog. My parents and I often went camping through the wild beauty of West Virginia, to places like Blackwater Falls State Park, where the air smelled of pine and river stone and the trees seemed older than time itself. We usually traveled with my grandparents, who would bring my cousin Mindy.

Mindy was a few years younger than I was and looked up to me completely. She adored my mother too. At the time, I only felt jealous of the attention she gave her. I did not yet understand what I would learn years later that Mindy longed for the kind of easy love my mother naturally gave. Looking back, I feel ashamed that my childish jealousy may have kept her from receiving more of that warmth when she needed it most.

Summers belonged mostly to another girl who would become the sister life forgot to officially assign me. Her name was Deanna, and she remains family to me even now. My parents bought her grandfather’s home and several acres of land, and there, one of my greatest childhood dreams came true: my father allowed me to have a horse of my own.

His name was Chief, an Appaloosa with fire in his veins. Before Chief, my dad had rented a twenty old Quarter Horse named Speedy to teach me how to ride. Speedy, despite his name, moved with all the urgency of a tired sofa but was excellent for my confidence. Chief was another matter entirely. Wild, proud, and quick to remind me who was in charge, he threw me more than once. But Chief gave me one of the first great lessons of life: the only real way to learn is to brush off the dirt, swallow the embarrassment, and climb back into the saddle.

I did not know then that life would soon test that lesson far beyond the pasture.

On another camping trip with my parents and grandparents, I was probably eleven years old, somewhere between fifth and sixth grade, still very much a child, though certain I was nearly grown. I always dressed carefully, with my mother’s help, and tried to look older than I was. In truth, I had braces, large teeth, scraggly hair, and all the awkwardness of that tender age. But I felt sophisticated in my favorite red clogs, fitted jeans, and the little outfits I had chosen for the trip.

I did not have many friends at school, so a campground full of strangers felt full of possibility. There were activities for children and teenagers, and I quickly met a group who invited me to an evening movie at the amphitheater. To me, it sounded glamorous, independence, adventurous, and just belonging.

When I asked my parents, they said absolutely not. It would be after dark, and I was not allowed to go. I was furious. I retreated to pout in private, nursing the indignity only an eleven-year-old can feel so deeply. Then I made a decision that felt bold and important at the time: I would go anyway.

It was still daylight when I slipped away. The group picked me up in a truck, the children piled laughing into the back while the adults rode in front. We drove farther than I expected through the enormous campground until we reached the amphitheater. By the time the movie began, the sky had already darkened, and with the darkness came fear. My excitement drained quickly. I knew I would be in terrible trouble. My father was strict, and I could already feel the weight of what awaited me.

So I decided to walk back alone.

At first it seemed manageable. Then the road grew darker, quieter, longer. Forest lined both sides. My red clogs struck the pavement in the silence “clop, clop, clop” the sound of a foolish little girl trying to outrun consequences. I no longer knew where I was.

Then a pickup truck slowed beside me. There were boys inside, older than I was, shouting things I could not fully hear or understand. Some instinct older than language rose inside me. I ran.

I ran blindly into the woods as they stopped the truck behind me. I heard doors slam, voices yelling, laughter cutting through the dark. Branches whipped my face. Twigs clawed at my arms. I lost my shoes somewhere in the panic and kept running barefoot, praying without words, driven by pure terror.

Then, like something conjured from mercy itself, I saw a house standing alone in the trees. I pounded on the door. A man opened it. He was the park ranger.

I was sobbing so hard I could barely speak, but somehow I told him enough. He brought me inside, calmed me, then put me in his truck and drove through the maze of roads until we found my campground. My parents were frantic. My grandparents were shaken. I had never seen fear on all their faces at once.

The ranger later went back to search for the boys but never found them. He checked on me the next day. I remember his kindness more clearly than any punishment that followed.

That night taught me more than obedience. It taught me that danger can arrive wearing laughter, that instincts are gifts, and that growing up has nothing to do with red clogs or pretending to be older than you are.

Long after the bruises faded and the scolding was forgotten, that night remained with me. Something invisible had shifted. Until then, the world had still seemed mostly kind, mostly manageable, a place where adults were near and consequences were simple. Afterward, I understood that shadows existed beyond the firelight, and that not every smile meant safety. But I also discovered something else: inside me lived a fierce instinct, quick and clear, that could rise when needed and guide me home. I moved differently after that night, more watchful, more aware, yet stronger too. Even now, many years later, I still walk through life with that same quiet radar, listening when something feels wrong, trusting the ancient voice within that once sent a barefoot girl running through the dark.

Room V
Becoming Visible

A Room of Transformation

Becoming Visible

Only one summer passed before the next room opened. I was no longer the girl in red clogs running barefoot through the dark, though I carried her with me, more watchful now, the way she had taught me to be. I had just finished sixth grade. Elementary school was behind me, and middle school was waiting that fall, though I didn’t know yet what either of those things would come to mean.

It was a different summer, a different lake.

That summer, the grass at the campground had gone dry and brittle in the heat, cracking under our bare feet as we ran toward the lake. I remember the shock of the cold water against the sun on my skin, how good it felt, how alive. I was there with Deanna and her cousin, on the shore, when we heard the shouting.

A group of teenage boys had been swimming nearby. One of them had eaten lunch not long before, then dove into the deep water with the others. I didn’t know him. I never even learned his name. But I watched as the men nearby tried to reach him, tried to pull him back to the surface, and failed. I watched the lake take him. Later they had to drag the water to bring him home.

I had never seen death arrive that close before. Sudden, senseless, completely indifferent to how young he was or how ordinary the afternoon had been until that moment. I think something in me understood, without yet having words for it, that life could simply end. That it asked no permission. That you could be laughing in the sun one minute and gone the next.

I did not go home from that summer unchanged.

I got braces. I got a haircut nobody in our town had yet, the kind you only saw in magazines, and the barber who gave it to me ended up with a line of new clients because of it. My mother, as she always did, looked forward to spending her hard earned paycheck to better my life. She took me on one of our trips to Columbus for new clothes for the year ahead. I started saying out loud, to anyone who would listen, that I was going to be a model. Not as a wish anymore. As a fact I hadn’t lived into yet.

My mother spent her money on me, on our home, on making our life full. My father saved every dollar he earned and kept it close, kept it his. Years later, when their marriage finally ended, she would let him take almost all of it. She was so broken by what he had done to her that she let him convince her it was somehow her fault, that she deserved nothing. She just wanted to be free of him. He had already taken far more than money. I learned something from watching that, something about what money is actually for. I have found in my own life that when it stops being the point, when you refuse to manipulate or use people to get it, it tends to find its way to you anyway. Enough of it, always.

I have always believed that what you speak begins to take shape. That summer, I think I started speaking my life into being.

By September, when I walked into Blennerhassett Junior High, a school far bigger than anything I’d known, full of faces from elementary schools I’d never set foot in, nobody there had ever seen the girl who used to cry herself to sleep over schoolyard laughter. They only saw who I had become. And somewhere inside that transformation, quietly, was a boy I never knew, whose death taught me without meaning to that there was no time left to wait to become myself.

Room VI
My Kindred Spirits

A Room of Belonging

My Kindred Spirits

I had barely found myself when I found them.

Scott I can still see on that stage, giving everything he had to a room in Parkersburg that had no idea what it was watching. He was a singer, a dancer, a natural performer with a genuinely beautiful smile and dark brown eyes that I still see clearly even now. He was too talented for that town, and somewhere deep down I think we both knew it. But he never let that knowledge make him bitter. He just kept performing, kept encouraging, kept finding ways to make the people around him feel better about who they were. He had a generous soul in the truest sense of the word, not generous the way people say it casually, but generous the way a person is when they genuinely cannot help wanting to lift others.

He came to my house almost every day after school. We would talk about our dreams, dress up, dance, and imagine the lives we were going to build somewhere far from West Virginia. My father would hear him in my bedroom and say something to my mother, his voice carrying that particular unease he had about things he didn’t understand. My mother would simply say, it’s Scott. And that was enough. She understood what my father couldn’t, or wouldn’t, that Scott was safe and loving and exactly the friend I needed.

Kelly was something else entirely. Where Scott and I were open-hearted and perhaps too willing to give ourselves away, Kelly was precise. She had no patience for ignorance, no interest in suffering fools, and absolutely no intention of sharing her best friend with anyone she deemed unworthy. She was the popular, funny cheerleader who could make an entire room laugh and then, in the same breath, cut someone down to size without a second thought. She was stubborn and picky and fiercely, possessively loyal.

Kelly had no great dreams of leaving. She knew exactly where she belonged, and it was there, in the town we grew up in, with the boy she loved. She married her high school sweetheart and is married to him still today. There is a wholeness in that, a certainty about herself that I have always quietly admired. She didn’t need the world to tell her who she was. She already knew.

And yet she supported Scott and me in our dreams completely, cheering us toward lives she had no desire to live herself. That is its own kind of love. Not everyone who believes in your dream needs to share it.

The three of us became inseparable. The three musketeers, we called ourselves, though we fought like it too. Kelly would watch Scott and me extend ourselves toward someone who didn’t deserve it, shake her head, and say we were wasting our time. Many times she was right. It didn’t change anything. Scott and I would pick ourselves up and try again. That was just who we were. Some things about a person don’t bend, no matter how many times life tries to teach them otherwise.

Scott was the kind of person who sang whether he was on a stage or not. He sang walking down the hall, sang at the lunch table, sang to himself in the middle of a conversation, and nobody minded, because his voice was genuinely beautiful and his joy was genuinely contagious. You couldn’t watch him and stay still. Something in his energy reached into you and lifted your feet. He made you want to live more fully just by showing you what living fully looked like.

He knew every word of every Barry Manilow song. I know this because I was sitting right beside him the night a small group of us went to Columbus to see him in concert, Kelly there too, and our teacher who had chosen us as her favorites. Scott sang the whole time. Not quietly, not under his breath, but fully, the way he did everything, with his whole chest and his whole heart. I think it was my first concert. I only remember being glad he was next to me.

What people didn’t always see, what I only saw in our quietest moments, was the place where all that brightness came from and what it cost him. His father was a hard man, strict in the way that leaves marks, and for Scott, who could not have been anyone other than who he was, those marks went deep. We never talked about it directly. We didn’t need to. His eyes told me when he was carrying something, just as mine told him. We held it for each other without words, without making it into something that had to be named or solved.

Our fathers, I understood much later, had more in common than we ever said out loud. Mine was not as harsh, but he had his own kind of distance. I remember being five or six years old, walking up to him while he was outside cutting the shrubbery, and asking, “Dad, do you love me as much as you love mommy?” He looked right at me and said, no. No explanation. No softer version. Just no. I think Scott’s father answered his son in much the same way… not always in words, but in that daily language of distance that certain men speak without knowing it.

Neither of us felt love from our fathers. Our mothers loved us with everything they had… and both marriages eventually broke under the weight of carrying us alone. We never said this to each other. But I think we knew it in our bodies long before we had words for it. It was part of why we could be so quiet together, the two of us, while everyone around us thought of Scott and Kelly as the loud ones, the theatrical ones, the ones who announced themselves. I was never that, not in public… but I have always liked to think I made the room brighter too, just in my own quieter way.

He just wanted to love and be loved. Like all of us.

It began with my mother.

Not with the agencies or the runways or the photographs that would come later, but with her, driving me to Columbus in my junior high years so I could attend the Barbizon School of Modeling. She paid for it herself. She made sure I went. I think she saw something in me before I had found it myself, or before I was willing to trust it, and she quietly put the tools in my hands without making a performance of it.

After Barbizon came Lexington. Every weekend, she drove me there, four hours each way, eight hours in a car in a single day, so I could train with the agency I had signed with in Kentucky. I spent most of those drives lying in the back seat, my hair in rollers, doing my makeup as we moved through the dark of the early morning, the radio playing whatever was playing in those early years of the eighties. I don’t know how long those drives were. They felt long and they also felt easy, the way time feels when you are moving toward something and you know it. My mother stayed at the agency while I worked, I think, and then she drove me home. That was our weekend, every weekend, for as long as it took.

She never made me feel it was a sacrifice.

All of it was moving toward Las Vegas in early October 1982, during homecoming week back in Parkersburg. I had just turned seventeen. There was a national competition, enormous, girls from everywhere across the country, so many that it seemed like thousands. I worked for it. I prepared the way I had learned to prepare, with seriousness and also with instinct, and when the time came to do my commercial, I found the thing I always find when I am nervous and fully committed at the same time. I found the humor in it.

The commercial was for a bra. The line was something like, if you want to make mountains out of molehills… and I turned back around, having changed underneath, and I had very large breasts. I had stuffed the bra. I don’t know exactly how I did it, only that I did, and it worked. The room responded the way a room responds when someone commits to something completely, without apology.

They called my name.

I remember that moment as the highest point of my young life. I had earned it myself, step by step, Lexington to Las Vegas, rollers in the back seat to a stage with a spotlight. Eileen Ford wanted to sign me. Zoli wanted me. A television agency from Los Angeles wanted me, Warner Brothers as well. But I chose Elite, John Casablancas, because he had girls who looked like me… more exotic, more of the world. That was where I belonged. Elite paid for my test shots, my composites, my first weeks in New York. They believed in me the way you want someone to believe in you, with their resources as well as their words.

I came home from Las Vegas carrying the news of my victory, ready to tell my father.

I think I imagined it in a particular way, the way you imagine things when you are young and have just done something extraordinary. I thought he might say congratulations. I thought this might finally be the moment.

As soon as I returned home, he told me he was leaving my mother. That he was in love with someone else. He told me before he told her.

My mother’s breakdown was not only about him. It was also about what lay ahead. She had stayed in that marriage for years, had seen lawyers, had threatened to leave, but never did. She stayed because of me. In October 1982 I was still home, but Las Vegas had made my departure real. I would leave for New York in April, and she had known for a long time that she would not stop me, that she loved me too much to ask me to stay. Her husband had just walked out, and within months her daughter would have to walk out into her own life too.

He took the wind out of my sails. That’s for sure. And my mother’s as well.

He had told me, when I chose modeling over finishing school, that he would take away the college fund he had saved for me. He did. He had always told me I would never make it, that this was not a life a person could build. I built it anyway. And years later, when it was clear that I had, he tried to tell a different story. He tried to say he had supported me.

He never did.

But my mother drove me to Columbus. My mother drove me to Lexington, Kentucky every weekend. Eight hours in a car, there and back, week after week, for as long as it took. My mother sat in a waiting room somewhere and believed, without needing to say it, that the girl in the back seat with rollers in her hair was going to make it.

She was right.

Room VII
The Ones Who Believed

A Room in the Concrete Jungle

The Ones Who Believed

When I arrived in New York in April 1983, the city was louder than anything I had ever known. Lights, horns, motion... a true concrete jungle, filled with thousands who had come, like me, to make their dreams real. I took the subway that first morning, the Lexington line from 86th Street down to 59th, butterflies filling my stomach as I came up into the daylight and walked past Bloomingdale’s toward 119 East 60th Street, between Park and Lexington. Above me, billboards flashed with the beautiful smiles of models, and I remember exactly what I was thinking as I walked beneath them. I was going from the Parkersburg billboards to the Big Apple ones. I had been on the small ones already. These were simply next.

Then I saw it above the door, big and bold: Elite. And the feeling that arrived was not excitement exactly. It was recognition. This was where I belonged.

The elevator opened and two models stepped out, portfolios in hand, and for one moment my certainty left me. They seemed so worldly. I felt so small town. But dreams shake off those insecurities, and that is just what I did. I walked in and gave the receptionist my name.

Waiting, I studied the walls... comp cards and Elite posters, all their girls looking down at me. I knew I would soon be up there too. Beyond the reception, the bookers worked their phones at long tables, models orbiting around them. This was real, and it was happening.

Then I was brought down the hall to meet John.

John Casablancas was handsome and worldly, with beautiful, smiling brown eyes and an accent that charmed everyone who heard it. I could see why so many women fell in love with him. But I was a girl, and even seeing his charms, neither of us saw anything in that room but how to make me a successful model for Elite. He never tried to do anything but lead me in the right direction, and that first day was exactly the same. He explained that I would begin testing with different photographers... I am sure it was already arranged before I ever sat down. I left his office feeling confident, feeling chosen. And every time I met with John over the years that followed, I felt the same thing: special, looked after, as if he truly cared about what became of me. Because he did.

The testing began almost at once. Among my first shoots were two French brothers, Dominique and Jacques Silberstein, well known in the New York scene of those years. I was too new to understand what their attention meant. But they were so confident in what they saw through their lens that they began telling everyone I would become a very successful model. Not to me... to everyone. The word was moving through the city before I had even learned my way around it.

And then there was the rainy day. I had a go-see with Lance Staedler, a photographer whose name I knew enough to be nervous about, and New York that day was under a terrible rain. I arrived soaked... my hair wild, whatever I had planned for my appearance completely undone. I walked in exactly as I was, because there was nothing else to do. Lance took one look at me, threw his own scarf around my neck, and shot. One photograph. I was booked for the job immediately.

I have thought about that picture many times over the years. Everything the industry would spend hours constructing... the hair, the styling, the mood... the rain had done for free, and a great photographer had the eye to leave it alone. It would take me years to understand that this was the lesson of my whole career, maybe of my whole life. The realness was the beauty. I never photographed better than when I was simply allowed to be exactly what I was.

By August, a few months after I had arrived in New York, John called me into his office. He had thought carefully about my next move, he told me, and he felt I should accept the contract being offered by Elite Tokyo: two months, with money guaranteed whether I worked or not. Twenty-five thousand dollars... perhaps thirty-five, I no longer remember the exact number, only that it was more money than a girl from Parkersburg could quite believe existed with her name on it.

But it was his reasoning that stayed with me all my life. With that money behind me, he explained, I could go to Europe afterward and choose my jobs. Never starving. Never taking just anything simply to survive. I would arrive in Paris and Milan free, and freedom in that world was everything. And beneath his words was something he was too kind to say plainly: he knew I was still a girl. He knew I needed time to mature. And he knew that Tokyo, of all the places he could send me, would keep me safe.

I have thought about that conversation so many times since. The most powerful man in modeling, who could have simply used me up as fast as the market would allow, instead sat across his desk and built my future patiently, like an architect. My own father had told me I would never make it. This man, who had known me a few months, quietly arranged my life so that I could.

I said yes. And a few weeks later I was on a plane to a country where I could not read a single sign... carrying a contract, my stuffed animals, and my Bible.

Room VIII
Where Silence Speaks

A Room Without Words

Where Silence Speaks

I left New York for Tokyo in August 1983, still seventeen years old. The flight seemed to last forever. When I finally landed at Narita International Airport, it was a Saturday evening, and waiting for me was a Japanese man who spoke almost no English. He drove me through the enormous glowing sprawl of Tokyo to the Roppongi neighborhood, to a small apartment where I would be living alone. I had signed a contract with Elite Tokyo for two months, guaranteed whether I worked or not... more money than I could believe, exactly as John had arranged. What no contract mentioned was everything else.

Upon entering the apartment, the man suddenly yelled at me in Japanese, motioning at my feet. I had no idea what I had done. He kept gesturing until I understood: my shoes. I was to remove my shoes. I did, and he turned and left without another word, and the door closed, and I was alone on the other side of the world.

I cried myself to sleep that first night. And when I woke the next morning, I truly understood that I had entered a completely new world, so unfamiliar and so foreign that I had no idea what was waiting for me. I think I was even afraid to look out the window. This was before cell phones, before email, before any thread I could pull to reach home. I spent that entire first weekend closed inside my little apartment, everything around me printed in a language I could not begin to read, until Monday morning when someone from the agency came to bring me in to meet everyone. Not long after I arrived, I celebrated my eighteenth birthday there, alone on the other side of the world and already living a life larger than anything I had known.

Then the work began, and it did not stop. I worked every single day of those two months. A driver and translator took me to every go-see and every job, because without them I could not have found my way or spoken a word. I tried to make friends with the other models in my building, but there was a distance between us that never closed. They were jealous that I had my own apartment and that I worked so much. So I was, in the truest sense, completely alone.

I was allowed to call my mother once a week from the agency. Once a week, a few minutes, across the whole planet. And I was not honest with her. She was going through the divorce, and she had already sacrificed everything for my dream. The last thing I was going to tell her was that I was lonely as hell in a place where I couldn’t even eat without pointing at a photograph, never knowing what would actually arrive at the table. There was a little noodle shop directly under my apartment with pictures on the menu, and I ate there almost every meal, pointing. On the jobs there were bento boxes, and I ate them out of pure necessity, too young and too overwhelmed to understand the extraordinary food culture I was standing in the middle of. It would take me years, and a whole life, to appreciate what Japan had been offering me all along.

One day I was working for Sony, a big job, seated at a huge table across from the most important people overseeing the shoot. At lunch, I stood my chopsticks straight up in my bowl of rice. The entire table erupted. Everyone was yelling, and once again I had no idea what I had done. The translator finally explained: chopsticks standing in rice is a funeral rite. I had, without knowing, wished death upon the man across from me. I never forgot it. To this day, I am very careful with my chopsticks.

What saved me in Tokyo had a name, and his name was Paul. He was the head of the agency... British, though he had lived in Japan so long he spoke the language fluently. An outsider who had made a home in a foreign world, and perhaps that is why he recognized me. He felt for me, and he was also glad to have me, since I was bringing in more than my contract promised. He and his wife began inviting me to their home, and soon, on the rare evenings he knew I was free, I would babysit their children.

Those evenings were the only moments in Tokyo that I felt a sense of belonging. The face of Elite Tokyo, working every day, and on her nights off she was putting someone else’s children to sleep... and it was the closest thing to home on that side of the earth. It was with Paul and his wife, in a restaurant, that I tried sushi for the first time. Today it is one of my favorite foods in the world. That night, I simply could not comprehend it. They tried so hard to enrich my Tokyo, to hand me the beauty of the place in a form I could hold. I have thought of them so many times since. I even tried to find Paul years later, but too much time had passed.

Near the end of my stay, when I knew I was leaving, Tokyo finally showed me its joy. A few of the girls from the agency and I spent an entire day shopping in the Ginza, the glittering heart of it all... though perhaps it was Harajuku; after all these years the neighborhoods blur softly in my memory. What I have never forgotten is what we found in the streets. It was a Sunday, when Tokyo closed its great avenues to cars, and there, in the middle of Japan, were dancers dressed head to toe in the American fifties. Leather jackets and pompadours. Poodle skirts and saddle shoes. Dancing and singing the old American rock and roll with complete seriousness and complete joy, the way the Japanese seemed to do everything, while crowds gathered around them clapping.

I stood there watching, eighteen years old and homesick down to my bones, while Tokyo sang my own country back to me. I couldn’t wait to go home... and it was one of the most fun days of my entire stay. Both things were true at once. That was Tokyo’s last lesson, I think. A place can be the loneliest you have ever known and still hand you, right at the end, a day of pure delight. It was as if the city were saying goodbye kindly, in the only language we shared. The music.

When the two months ended, I could not wait to leave. And now, all these years later, I would give a great deal to return. That is what Tokyo taught me, though the lesson took decades to arrive: sometimes we are given a world before we are ready to receive it. I had arrived at seventeen and turned eighteen there, and I survived it... the silence, the aloneness, the yelling I never understood, the meals I pointed at. The girl who lay in her grandmother’s garden imagining her way into other worlds had finally landed in one, and it was harder than the ants ever showed her. But she stayed. She worked every day. She found the one warm room in the city and let it hold her.

And she left with exactly what John had promised: freedom. Europe was next, and this time, I would arrive able to choose.

Room IX
Hamburg, Spring 1984

A Room Beside the Alster

Hamburg, Spring 1984

After returning to New York from Tokyo, Europe finally called, and my first stop was Hamburg. I was eighteen years old. I could not have known it then, but my first step onto European soil was a German one... and Germany, in time, would come to mean more in my life than I could possibly have imagined that spring.

I was with Parker-Sed, the agency run by Dorothee Parker and Sebastian Sed. The office was in one of those elegant historic villas near the Alster, with high ceilings, polished floors, tall windows, and the quiet dignity that Hamburg seemed to carry so naturally. I still remember walking through that grand front door with my portfolio in hand, feeling very young and very far from home.

Dorothee Parker had a distinguished presence, refined and almost cinematic. Every day I would collect my casting slips and go out into the city with my portfolio and my Sedcard, never imagining then that Sebastian Sed had created the very format every model in the world would one day carry. To me it was simply part of the ritual. New city. New agency. New castings. New faces deciding what they saw when they looked at mine.

And always, the Polaroids. At castings, at bookings, before shoots, after hair, after makeup, before the real film began. Click. Front. Click. Profile. Click. Turn your head. Click. Smile less. Smile more. Those little instant photographs were everywhere in my life as a model. They were not glamorous. They were proof of how you looked that day, in that light, with that hair, in that room. Long before digital screens, they were how everyone checked the truth of the moment. I still have a few. Sometimes I wonder where all the others went.

The models apartment was nearby, around Milchstraße, in another beautiful white villa close to the Alster. It was the first time I lived in a mixed apartment, male and female models together, and once again I was not exactly popular. I do not suppose the stuffed animals and the Bible helped. The others had already formed their little world, and I was not invited very far inside it.

So I walked. I walked around the Alster, through the parks, past the old villas and the white sailboats moving across the water. I walked through Milchstraße and the hidden courtyards of Pöseldorf, those secret little passages where a plain doorway could suddenly open into cobblestones, ivy, music, a restaurant, a bar, another world tucked behind the first one. Hamburg was beautiful in a quiet, elegant way, and spring made it even softer.

One of my favorite places was The Cliff, a little place out on the water where I could sit with something to drink and watch the sailboats on the Alster. I do not remember feeling lonely there. I remember feeling peaceful. In the apartment I may have been the girl outside the circle, but beside the water I belonged to myself.

One night the others convinced me to come along with them. They said we were going to see a show, but they did not say what kind... and I believe now that was the point. They took me to the Reeperbahn and into Pulverfass. They were waiting for the girl with the Bible to be scandalized.

But I was not scandalized. I was enchanted. The costumes, the performances, the wit, the music, the confidence, the sheer joyful artistry of it all... it was spectacular. I have never forgotten that night, and I would not trade it. If it was meant as a joke on me, it became a gift instead. That happened to me often in life. People kept trying to embarrass me with the world, and I kept falling in love with it instead.

What I loved most in Hamburg, though, was still the quiet. The Alster. The trees. The walks. The feeling that I could be alone and still be held by something beautiful. Tokyo had taught me to survive solitude. Hamburg taught me to enjoy it. Somewhere between those two cities, being alone stopped being loneliness and became my own good company, and that has never left me.

Room X
Munich, Early Summer 1984

The Angel of Peace

Munich, Early Summer 1984

There are songs that belong to a season of life rather than an album. Whenever I hear Nena singing 99 Luftballons, I am no longer wherever I happen to be. I am eighteen years old again. I have my Walkman on. The morning air is cool. Fresh bread is baking somewhere nearby. And I am walking through Munich.

When Louisa Models called from Munich, I packed my suitcase once again and climbed aboard another train. Germany no longer felt entirely foreign. Hamburg had quietly changed me. I had learned enough German to order breakfast without panicking, enough confidence to board trains without feeling lost, and enough independence to believe that perhaps I belonged in this remarkable country after all. Hamburg had even taught me to enjoy my own company. I had no idea Munich was waiting to teach me something entirely different. I had no idea I was about to discover one of the cities that would quietly remain in my heart for the rest of my life.

Louisa Models was located in the elegant neighborhood of Bogenhausen, where grand old villas stood behind wrought-iron gates, tree-lined streets curved gently through quiet neighborhoods, and everything seemed touched by an effortless sense of order and beauty. Every morning I walked toward the agency with my Walkman playing. More often than not, it was Nena’s 99 Luftballons. Even today, I don’t simply hear that song. I walk back into Munich.

Our modeling pension was nothing extravagant. It was something much better. It was home. An older German Hausfrau cared for the house with the quiet dignity of someone who believed that breakfast was one of the purest ways to care for another human being. Every morning she transformed the dining room into something beautiful. Fresh Brötchen still warm from the neighborhood bakery. Dense rye breads. Perfectly soft-boiled eggs waiting patiently in delicate porcelain egg cups. Platters of cheeses and thinly sliced German meats. Fresh yogurt, muesli, bowls of fruit, rich coffee served in the most beautiful floral porcelain cups I had ever seen. To this day, I can still picture those cups.

Funny how memory works. I cannot remember the names of most of my bookers. Many of the girls have faded into soft silhouettes. Photographers I once thought unforgettable have disappeared into time. Yet I have never forgotten her. When work slowed and money became scarce, she somehow always knew. Without ever making me feel embarrassed, she would quietly leave fruit in my room. An apple. A banana. Something simple. Enough so I would never go hungry later that day. At eighteen, I accepted it as kindness. Today I understand it as love.

The English Garden became one of my favorite places in Munich. My friend and I spent many afternoons wandering its endless green paths before finding our favorite tables at the Seehaus Beer Garden beside the Kleinhesseloher Lake. I can still almost see her face, though her name has escaped me after all these years. Sometimes we went to the Chinese Tower instead. Everywhere there were joggers, music, laughter, giant Bavarian pretzels, and impossibly tall glasses of Franziskaner Weißbier carried effortlessly by servers dressed in traditional lederhosen and beautiful dirndls. I remember watching them weave through crowded tables balancing enormous trays, never spilling a single drop. It looked like choreography.

The English Garden also gave me one of my first true culture shocks. People sunbathed completely nude. Not hidden away. Simply lying in the sunshine as though it were the most ordinary thing in the world. Occasionally someone would stroll toward the tram wearing nothing but a pair of shoes. Coming from Parkersburg, West Virginia, I could hardly believe what I was seeing. The remarkable thing wasn’t the nudity. It was that nobody seemed to notice. There was no embarrassment. No staring. No scandal. Just people enjoying a beautiful afternoon. It fascinated me.

One afternoon my friend confidently announced that she had discovered the secret to the perfect suntan. Butter. Not tanning oil. Butter. So we quietly borrowed fresh butter from our wonderful Hausfrau’s breakfast table and enthusiastically covered ourselves with it before lying out in the Bavarian sunshine. It worked. Just not in the way we had imagined. We became spectacularly sunburned. Our skin turned bright red. We were sick for days. We couldn’t work. The agency was furious. Looking back now, I can’t help but laugh. At the time, every movement hurt too much to laugh very hard. Needless to say, I never used butter as sunscreen again.

I didn’t go out often. I was still that quiet, timid girl from Parkersburg. But the agency occasionally organized evenings at Munich’s legendary clubs, and for a few magical nights I stepped into a completely different world, where models, musicians, actors, and football stars all seemed to gather under the same flashing lights. It was exciting. But somehow, those glittering nights are not what stayed with me.

What stayed with me were the mornings. Walking beneath the trees. The Angel of Peace watching over the city, golden above the river. Window shopping along Maximilianstraße. Enjoying a Wiener Schnitzel at Käfer. Peeling the skin from my first Weißwurst before dipping it into sweet Bavarian mustard. Simple moments. Beautiful moments. The ones that quietly become part of you forever.

One weekend my friend and I decided to take the train from München Hauptbahnhof to Venice. We were young. We had very little money. That never seemed to matter. We stayed in a tiny convent run by Italian nuns because it was all we could afford. The rooms were spotless, inexpensive, and came with one non-negotiable rule: be home before curfew. Naturally… we weren’t.

After meeting two charming Italian men, we accepted an invitation to dinner on the Lido, assuring them we were staying at a hotel instead of admitting we had a strict convent curfew. Returning that night no longer seemed possible. So we did what eighteen-year-old girls sometimes do. We improvised. We found one of the canvas beach cabanas and decided to sleep there until morning before catching the first train back to Munich. It sounded wonderfully adventurous over dinner. It was considerably less romantic after midnight. We were freezing. We wrapped ourselves in every piece of clothing we had brought from Munich and spent most of the night laughing at our own ridiculous plan.

Just before sunrise I remember staring out at the Adriatic Sea, convinced for one brief moment that I had spotted a shark’s fin cutting through the water. Of course, it was almost certainly not a shark. It was probably a dolphin, driftwood, or simply my imagination. But Jaws had left its mark on an entire generation, and for one sleepy moment I believed it. We laughed even harder. That chilly night remains one of the happiest memories of my youth.

Early the next morning we boarded the vaporetto back toward Venice’s Santa Lucia station, still cold, slightly sandy, wonderfully exhausted, and somehow feeling richer than when we had arrived. Returning to Munich that evening felt like coming home. And waiting there, as always, was another beautiful breakfast.

As I write these words today, I realize something I never understood then. For years I thought I was simply the kind of person who packed food for everyone. Whenever Brando’s friends visited our home, they never left empty-handed. If someone was driving back to Los Angeles, I quietly packed fruit, a bottle of water, perhaps a granola bar, just enough so they wouldn’t be hungry along the way. It became a family joke. I thought it was simply part of who I was. Only now, while writing these pages, do I understand where it began.

Somewhere in Munich, an older German woman quietly taught me that love is often expressed in the smallest gestures. An apple. A banana. A beautiful breakfast. A kindness offered without making someone feel they needed it. Munich has a golden angel standing high above the Isar, watching over the city. But the angel I remember best wore an apron, and had her table set before the rest of the house was awake.

Perhaps that is why I have always been able to make a home wherever life has taken me. Home was never only a place. It became something I quietly carried with me. And perhaps… I have been carrying her breakfast table with me ever since.

Room XI
London, 1984

True

London, 1984

Somewhere between Munich and London, the tape changed. Nena faded. A saxophone began. Spandau Ballet was singing True, and though I could not have known it then, that song was going to hold an entire city for me.

London wasn’t part of the original plan. By the time I left Munich, I had gained a little weight. Looking back, it wasn’t much by ordinary standards, but in the modeling world of 1984, ordinary didn’t exist. I remember John Casablancas seeing me and saying something that has stayed with me all these years. “Monica, you can’t gain weight. You’re not one of the girls who are five-foot-ten or six feet tall. You’re five-foot-eight. You have to stay very slim.” It wasn’t said to hurt me. It was simply the reality of the business. Then he said something that changed my next chapter. “I’m sending you to London.” And just like that, I found myself beginning another life.

Models 1 became my London agency. Their apartment was in the Barbican, not the Barbizon as I had remembered for years. Funny how memory works. One word leads to another until the right door finally opens.

The Barbican felt unlike anywhere I had ever been. It was all concrete terraces, elevated walkways, and strange modern buildings that looked almost futuristic to a young American girl. The apartment buzzed with models coming and going at every hour, portfolios leaning against walls, maps of London folded into handbags, everyone chasing castings somewhere across the city.

Slowly, London began to belong to me. I learned the Underground. I wandered Oxford Street and Bond Street. I walked for hours. And always, that song. Whenever I hear True, I am back on those pavements. I can almost feel the warm London air again. I remember walking without any particular destination, dreaming that one day I would find a true love of my own. Isn’t it funny how a single song can preserve an entire season of your life?

Because modeling had taken the place of school, I tried to give myself the education I was missing. I have always been an avid reader, and especially in that time I read everything I could get my hands on. Books were my classrooms, and London was full of them.

One building I never forgot was the Senate House, the towering white tower that inspired George Orwell’s Ministry of Truth. Though 1984 would not have been my cup of tea... as the English would say... I loved knowing it was there. It was the year 1984. I was walking past the Ministry of Truth with a song called True playing in my ears. I didn’t notice the rhyme then. I notice it now.

One of the most unexpected gifts London gave me was Meriel McCooey, the legendary fashion editor at The Sunday Times Magazine. Somehow she saw something in me beyond another model’s composite card. She trusted me.

Meriel lived alone, and everything about her home said so. It was entirely hers, filled with her books and her taste, her quiet collections, her own aesthetic world. She spent her days making the most beautiful women in the world look spectacular, yet she never wanted her own photograph taken. All of her brilliance went outward, onto the page, onto us.

That trust became extraordinary when she handed me the keys to her apartment while she went away. I believe it was in Mayfair, or very close by. What I remember most wasn’t the address but the view. Looking out over Hyde Park, I found myself drawn once again to trees and open space... nature, once again, in another concrete jungle. It seems I’ve always searched for little pieces of the natural world, no matter where I lived.

Working with Meriel led to one of the highlights of my months in London. We traveled to Marbella, Spain, to photograph the entire swimsuit edition for The Sunday Times Magazine. I was the only model on the shoot. We stayed at the legendary Marbella Club, where the days felt less like work and more like stepping into another world of blue sea, white umbrellas, golden light, and endless sunshine. Marbella was her world, and for a little while she let me live in it. For a young girl who had grown up in West Virginia, it hardly seemed real.

There were glittering nights in London too. Tramp was not far away, tucked behind an unassuming door, small enough that everyone was practically shoulder to shoulder. One night David Bowie was there, simply there, the way extraordinary things kept happening around me in those months. But as in Munich, the glitter is not what stayed. What stayed was a lesson about trust.

During that time I became friends with another successful model, Jinsey Dauk. She had Vogue covers. She was older and wiser than me, and I admired her. When she needed somewhere to stay for a few days, I invited her to stay in Meriel’s apartment without thinking twice. To me, it felt natural. Models looked after one another. We all knew what it was like to arrive in a foreign city with nowhere to go.

But I hadn’t understood what that apartment was. It wasn’t simply a place to sleep. It was her sanctuary, the one room in her life that belonged to no one else. The keys she had given me were not keys at all. They were the most private thing she had. She was deeply hurt. Our friendship did not survive it.

I never meant to betray her confidence, but intentions don’t always erase consequences. It remains one of those painful moments where kindness and trust collided, leaving two people with very different understandings of the same event. I was learning that trust, like the song said, was a word with more inside it than I knew.

Not all of my London memories revolve around fashion. My mother came to visit me, and together we explored the city. We did everything visitors are supposed to do, and we loved every bit of it. Harrods. The red double-decker buses. The luxurious black taxi cabs. The red telephone booths. London Bridge and Big Ben. And of course Buckingham Palace, with its guards mounted upon those gorgeous horses, so still they might have been painted.

Below our hotel was an Indian restaurant, and there my mother made one of the great discoveries of her life. She fell completely in love with Indian food. She would eat the spicy dishes until her hazel eyes turned green, tears rolling down her cheeks, laughing, determined to finish every bite. My son’s eyes do the very same thing when he eats his spicy food. Some things travel quietly through a family, waiting to reappear.

Eventually we rented a car. She drove. I refused. The thought of driving on the opposite side of the road terrified me, so my brave mother took the wheel while we headed toward Wales.

Somewhere near a little village along the sea, we found a white manor house that had become a hotel. I remembered it for years as a castle. My mother says it wasn’t. Memory builds its own architecture.

A lone man checked us in. We drove to the nearby town for a bite to eat, and when we returned, the hotel was empty. No one at the desk. No sounds. Just our skeleton key, the stairs, and a long corridor to our room. The room itself was very English and very modest. A double bed, two side tables, a telephone, a large table and chairs by the door. And, most peculiar to us, a sink in the middle of the room. Later, that sink would make sense.

That night I needed the bathroom, which meant the long hallway, eerily lit with small lights, to the WC at the far end. The moment I sat down, the light went off. I made a very quick exit. Running back down the hall, I saw that the door across from our room was standing open. I don’t know why, but I moved closer. Just as my hand touched the door, a whoosh of bone-chilling cold air passed straight through me. I froze for an instant. Only an instant. Then I tore into our room and slammed the door.

I told my mother, and we both agreed the sink now had sense. It was not just for washing hands and brushing teeth. Neither of us was walking that corridor again before morning. That night, the alarm clock on the side table went off at four in the morning. We had not set it.

At breakfast we were the only people in the dining room. There was a typical Welsh breakfast, though all I distinctly remember is the toast, standing upright in its silver rack, already cold and perfectly crisp. It seemed so very British.

We found the man who had checked us in and told him about the open door across from our room. He told us we were the only guests in the hotel. Then he told us that room was known for having a ghost. He said it the way you might mention that the sun was shining outside. Only then did my mother mention the woman she had seen on the landing of the staircase when we checked in. That, he said, was also a resident ghost.

We decided to cut our stay short. When my mother reached into her purse for the car keys, exactly where she had left them, they were gone. We searched the room. We searched the car. Nothing. Frantic, we went back up to the room a second time. The keys were lying in the middle of the bed. Coincidence or not, we were ready to say goodbye to that hotel. My mother drove us back toward London, on the wrong side of the road, perfectly calm.

I have looked for that white house for forty years and never found it. Perhaps some places prefer not to be found.

I went to London dreaming of a true love. I walked miles of pavement with that song in my ears, certain he was somewhere ahead of me. It took me a long time to see what London had actually given me. A stranger who handed me her keys. A friendship that taught me what trust costs. A hallway of cold air that no one has ever explained, and no one needed to. And beside me through all of it, laughing through tears at a curry house, taking the wheel when I was afraid, carrying a ghost quietly so I could sleep... the truest love I had, long before I knew to count it.

The song was right after all. I simply didn’t know yet where to look.

A Room for Two

My Mother’s Turn

By the end of 1984, I had returned from Tokyo to New York and then gone on to Hamburg, Munich, London, and Milan. Only after those European chapters did I return to be with my mother in Parkersburg, because she had made a decision, and I intended to be part of it.

She was still only in her late thirties, and she was going to make a new life for herself. She was absolutely right to do it. He had taken nearly everything, so there was not much to pack... a few things of hers, a few things of mine, and Misty, her beautiful prize-winning cat.

We chose Chicago together.

New York never interested my mother. She wanted a smaller, friendlier city, and Chicago had Elite, under Marie Anderson, where I could work. At the end of 1984, the two of us packed our little pile of belongings and headed west. We stayed first at the Claridge Hotel on Dearborn, and before long we had an apartment at 1221 North Dearborn, in the very heart of the city.

A new world was beginning... hers out of an ending, mine out of a becoming.

I will never forget how quickly she moved. It must have been a weekend when we arrived, because she bought the thick Sunday paper and immediately began circling job listings.

In January 1985, she began her new job at Liedtke Graphic downtown.

She walked down into the Clark/Division Red Line station, a woman from Parkersburg, West Virginia, descending into a world of concrete, steel, graffiti, and the unmistakable metallic screech of the CTA trains. It must have felt enormous. She rode into the city alone, interviewed for a job in a place where she knew almost no one, and by the time she came home that afternoon, she had been hired.

Of course she had.

My mother had been the Purchasing Director for the City of Parkersburg. She was intelligent, capable, and quietly fearless. She would remain with Frank Liedtke for years until the day he rewarded her loyalty with an early retirement, fully paid, because she had become one of the people he trusted most.

That was my mother.

Give her one Monday morning and a subway token, and she would build you a life.

I did my part too, in my own small way. Around the corner from our apartment was a beautiful gym where I found a part-time job folding towels and handing them to members as they arrived.

It remains the only ordinary job I have ever had, the only paycheck that came to me without an agency attached. I didn’t keep it long, but I’ve always been quietly proud of it. The girl who had once been the face of Elite Tokyo was happily folding towels because every dollar mattered. We were building a home together.

The real work continued at Elite Chicago. I built my portfolio one photograph at a time, growing into the model John Casablancas had patiently believed I could become. It was there I shot one of my first romantic editorials with a male model. There was no romance between us, only a camera and a story waiting to be told.

I was becoming.

Little by little, Chicago became ours.

On days when I wasn’t working, I would slip on my headphones, tuck my Walkman beneath my arm, and disappear into the city. Prince became the soundtrack of that winter. Walking north from our apartment, When Doves Cry blasting through my headphones, the cold Chicago wind whipped my gray wool scarf across my face as I admired the elegant brownstones and the quiet opulence of Astor Street. A sudden burst of sunlight briefly thawed my frozen cheeks before disappearing once again behind the heavy November sky.

Ahead, the icy waves of Lake Michigan crashed against the shoreline. I thought about walking closer but decided against the bitter wind, pulling my scarf tighter instead as I crossed the frozen lawns of Lincoln Park.

Then I saw it.

The Victorian silhouette of the Lincoln Park Conservatory rose before me like a glittering palace of glass against the slate-gray sky.

Almost on cue, the soaring guitar solo from Purple Rain filled my ears.

More than anything, I wanted warmth. I wanted the smell of damp earth.

I opened the heavy outer doors just as the song ended.

Silence.

There were no admission tickets then. No security guards. No turnstiles. No gift shop waiting at the entrance. Only beauty quietly welcoming you inside.

I opened the little plastic door of my Walkman, turned the cassette over, and pressed play.

“Dearly beloved... we are gathered here today... to get through this thing called life...”

As Prince’s voice filled my headphones, I stepped into the great Palm House and was met by a rush of tropical warmth. Humidity settled onto my skin. The scent of wet soil and growing plants surrounded me.

I had found a place where the world became wonderfully quiet.

I would sit on one of the benches until eventually I turned the music off altogether.

The silence was even more beautiful.

My breathing slowed. I listened to the soft drip... drip... drip... of condensation falling from the iron framework above. Here, no one expected anything from me. I didn’t have to explain myself. I could simply be.

Looking back now, I realize this became one of the quiet themes of my life. Whenever the world became too loud, I instinctively searched for places that asked nothing of me except my presence. My grandmother’s back steps. Paul’s living room in Tokyo. And now this glass palace in Lincoln Park, dripping quietly in the winter cold. The world kept offering me rooms within rooms, and I kept stepping inside.

Chicago slowly gave us our own little rituals.

My mother and I adored Edwardo’s Natural Pizza, where we always ordered the stuffed spinach pizza, rich, comforting, and unmistakably Chicago. Although the original restaurant has disappeared, I love knowing that behind Eduardo’s Enoteca the original kitchen still quietly prepares the same stuffed spinach pizza from its secret menu. Somehow that makes me smile. A small piece of our Chicago still survives.

Almost every morning, or sometimes late at night, I found myself at Winchell’s Donut House on Division and Dearborn, leaving with a glazed doughnut or a bear claw wrapped in wax paper. It was there that a homeless woman once refused the doughnut I offered her and shouted at me. I never forgot her face, or how quickly kindness can be misunderstood.

Just down the street stood the twenty-four-hour Walgreens my mother and I visited almost daily. Beneath its bright fluorescent lights and familiar blue-and-red sign, it became our neighborhood grocery store, pharmacy, and late-night comfort all at once.

One of my favorite places in the building was the rooftop pool and sauna. After long walks through the winter cold, I would ride the elevator to the penthouse level and step into the dry warmth of the sauna until the chill finally left my bones. Sometimes I floated quietly on my back beneath the spectacular retractable glass roof, watching the Chicago sky drift overhead, blue, gray, or filled with stars.

Peace came easily there.

Music seemed to follow me everywhere that year. One evening, after a modeling job, I was invited to Limelight, one of Chicago’s most exciting nightclubs. I was nineteen years old, dressed up, and feeling wonderfully grown up, as though I had truly stepped into this glamorous new world. During the evening I was introduced to Dave Stewart of the Eurythmics. I loved their music... Sweet Dreams was already one of my favorite songs... but celebrity itself had never impressed me very much. Almost immediately I felt uncomfortable. It wasn’t one particular thing I could explain. It was simply a feeling. His attention became more persistent than I wanted, and the more he pursued the conversation after I had politely declined, the more certain I became that I didn’t want to be there. So I left.

Looking back, I’m grateful I listened to that quiet inner voice. Throughout my life there would be many moments when something inside me whispered, This isn’t for you. It was usually right.

At night another sound became part of our lives. From the alley below came the steady crash of empty glass bottles being tossed into dumpsters behind Butch McGuire’s and the other lively bars along Division Street. And threading through it, something older and more beautiful: the clop, clop, clop of horse-drawn carriages moving up and down Dearborn, carrying their passengers through the lamplit streets on tours of the city. Those horses were magnificent, and the sound of their hooves on the stone became as much a part of our nights as the music and the laughter and the taxis. I don’t believe the carriages run there anymore. But I hear them still, in certain quiet moments, when I let Chicago come back to me. The neighborhood pulsed with all of it... music, laughter, taxis, people wandering home long after midnight. At first the noise kept us awake. Soon it became strangely comforting. It reminded us that life was happening all around us. And it was time for us to begin making ours happen too.

So there we were: my mother, Misty, and me... for a little while, anyway. Two women from Parkersburg in the heart of Chicago, each building her new life with her own hands, one Sunday newspaper, one subway ride, one portfolio, and one ordinary day at a time.
